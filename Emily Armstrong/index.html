Born in Los Angeles, Emily Armstrong came up in a city already saturated with rock history but her approach never leaned on nostalgia. Early on, she connected with guitarist Siouxsie Medley, and the two built what would become Dead Sara a project rooted in heavy riffs, sharp dynamics, and a refusal to clean things up too much. From the start, the focus wasn't image. It was sound, and how far it could be pushed without breaking. More recently, Armstrong stepped into a new role as a vocalist for Linkin Park a shift that brought her voice into a very different scale without changing the core of how she performs.



DEAD SARA

The band formed in the mid 2000s, playing small venues across Los Angeles before gaining wider attention in the early 2010s. Their self titled debut, Dead Sara, introduced a mix of hard rock, alternative, and punk influence driven by Armstrong's vocal range, which could shift from controlled to explosive without warning. Tracks like Weatherman became a defining moment, not because they were polished, but because they weren't. Follow up releases, including Pleasure to Meet You and Ain't It Tragic, kept that direction intact loud, direct, and unconcerned with trends.

LINKIN PARK

Joining Linkin Park placed Armstrong in a very different context one of the most recognized rock bands of the 21st century, with a global audience and a legacy shaped by Chester Bennington. Rather than imitating what came before, her role leans into contrast. The same rawness that defines her work in Dead Sara carries over, but against a more structured, large scale sound. It's less about replacement, more about evolution.
